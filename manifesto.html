College sports
belongs to the fans.

"The Fan's Network was built on a simple belief: the people who love the game should have a voice in its future."

For over a century, college sports has been unlike anything else in American life. It is not just entertainment. It is identity. It is community. It is the thread that connects generations of families to a place, a team, a tradition that means something beyond wins and losses.

That thread belongs to the fans. Not to television networks. Not to conference offices. Not to the financial models of private equity firms looking for the next asset to optimize. The fans built this. Town by town, stadium by stadium, generation by generation.

The Fan's Network — XSEN — exists to serve those fans. To give every fan base in America a home, a voice, and a direct connection to the athletes they support. Starting with the schools that need it most.

What's At Stake
"Outside investors are circling college sports with billion-dollar proposals. Fans have no seat at that table. Nobody asked them."

Right now, in boardrooms in Los Angeles and New York, private equity firms are actively pitching plans to restructure college football around financial returns. The proposals are sophisticated. The money is real. And the model is straightforward: consolidate the sport's most valuable programs into a closed league, centralize media rights, and extract returns for investors.

Seventy schools in. Sixty schools out. The programs left behind — and the fan bases who have supported them for generations — are simply not part of the calculation. They don't fit the financial model. Their loyalty, their decades of support, their communities — none of it shows up on a term sheet.

Meanwhile the schools inside the proposed structure face a different problem. Athletic departments are carrying hundreds of millions in debt. The House v. NCAA settlement requires them to share revenue directly with athletes — a welcome development for players, but a financial pressure that is pushing programs toward outside capital with strings attached.

Private equity doesn't invest without expecting control. And control of college sports — of scheduling, of media rights, of which programs survive and which are left behind — has never belonged to investors. It has belonged to the communities that built it.

The fans who drove eight hours to a bowl game. The alumni who named their children after legendary coaches. The towns whose entire identity is wrapped up in a Saturday afternoon in October. These are the real stakeholders of college sports. And right now they have no organized voice, no platform, and no seat at the table where the sport's future is being decided.

That is what The Fan's Network exists to change.

What We Believe
01

Every fan base deserves a network.

College sports in America spans over 130 programs — from the storied powerhouses of the Power Four to the passionate communities surrounding mid-major programs that have been part of their regions for generations.

Every one of those fan bases deserves infrastructure. A place to gather. An AI companion that knows their team's history. A live radio stream on game day. A real-time connection to the athletes they support. Not just the schools with billion-dollar TV deals — every school. Every fan.

02

Fan dollars should reach the athletes.

For the first time in history, fans have the ability to support college athletes directly — through NIL, through micro-donations, through subscriptions that flow real dollars to real people who pour everything into their sport.

The Fan's Network is built to make that happen. When a fan subscribes to their school's XSEN channel, a portion of that subscription supports NIL opportunities for that school's athletes. Not a corporation's shareholders. Not a media company's quarterly earnings. The athletes.

03

The network belongs to the fans who build it.

XSEN is not built for fans. It is built by fans, with fans, for fans. Every channel that launches is a community that takes shape around the people who show up — who share, who engage, who bring their decades of passion to a platform that finally gives it a home.

The Fan's Network grows because fans grow it. That is the only kind of network worth building.

A Note from the Founder

I've been a Sooner fan since I was a kid. I started attending games as a student at OU in 1978 — the year Billy Sims won the Heisman. I lived in Lincoln Hall first, a dorm sitting directly across the street from the south end zone, literally in the shadow of Memorial Stadium. Then Walker Tower. Then Adams Hall. I didn't miss a single home game the entire time I was a student. On game days, the roar didn't come from a broadcast — it came through the walls. I've followed this program through every era since. I was in the stands for bowl games across three decades, and I was there in person for the last time Oklahoma won a national championship. That was 2000. I'm still waiting on the next one.

I built XSEN for a simple reason: for the first time in history, the average fan could put real money directly into the hands of college athletes through NIL — and nobody had built the infrastructure to make that easy, personal, and connected to the school you actually love. That was the business. That was the mission.

Then I started reading about Project Rudy. About private equity firms circling college football. About schools taking on hundreds of millions in debt and looking to Wall Street for the exit ramp. About a proposed super league that would decide — from a boardroom — which 70 programs matter and which 60 don't.

Later in life — semi-retired and apparently a glutton for punishment — I sat down and passed the Series 6, 63, and 65 financial exams — the federal licensing tests that qualify you to sell securities and act as a registered investment adviser. First try, each one. I also hold active life and health insurance licenses in New Mexico, Oklahoma, and Texas. First try on those too. Not bad for a C student at OU. I understand how capital markets work. I understand what private equity firms do with assets they acquire. I understand the difference between a fiduciary obligation to investors and a genuine obligation to a community.

That's when XSEN became something bigger than a NIL platform. If fans were already the reason any of this exists — the ticket sales, the TV deals, the jerseys, all of it — then fans deserved a voice in what comes next. Not just a seat in the stadium. A seat at the table.

The Fan's Network is how we take it.

— Kevin Whitaker
Founder, Peak Platforms
